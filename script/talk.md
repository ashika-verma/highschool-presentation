# Talk Script

**Audience:** High school women interested in coding (Girls Who Code)
**Format:** 20 minutes over Zoom — students are together in one classroom in Texas, presenter is in NYC
**Vibe:** Honest, fun, human. Not a TED talk. Not a recruiting pitch.

## 0:00 — The Opening

> "Okay — I see some of you have already found the app. Go ahead, pick whatever color you want."

> "I've always had a complicated relationship with light switches."

_Let that land weird. Then:_

> "When I was your age I was lying in bed one summer and I did not want to get up to turn off the lights. The switch was all the way across the room. So I went to the garage, grabbed cardboard, tape, a cassette player, a clip, some string, and a ball — and spent the next three hours building a machine where rolling the ball down a ramp would flip the switch from my bed."
>
> "It was clunky. I had to reset it every time I walked in. But it worked. And I was genuinely proud of it."
>
> "That's basically my whole life. I see I want to do or broken, I refuse to accept it, and I build my own version. Usually ridiculous. Sometimes useful."
>
> "My name is Ashika. I went to CSHS, then MIT, and now I'm in New York. And what y'all are doing to my apartment right now is a much cleaner version of that cardboard machine."

## 2:00 — Your Story

> This is a girls who code event, so I want to tell you guys how it started for me especially as a woman in college station. which it happens to begin in middle school.

TURN ON SLIDE DECK

> I took robotics. I really wanted to be the one who programmed the robot — I wanted to look like that cool edgy hacker-type woman I'd see on TV. What I didn't know: so did every other kid in the class. Twenty other guys, all fighting for time on the one computer."

> I was bummed for a while. So what can I actually get my hands on?"

> My TI 84. Those were everywhere — every classroom, always in my backpack, and there was a programming setting on it. I taught myself the basics from YouTube. And the first thing I built was a prank."

> "If you typed in a calculation, it'd give you an answer that was... almost right. Randomly off by somewhere between negative five and positive five. Just enough to make you second-guess yourself."

> "I showed it to my friend Ben. He was impressed — so he built something to impress me back. So I built something else. We started getting into each other's calculators and writing programs to trip each other up. It kept escalating. Eventually we each had to build a lock screen just so the other one couldn't get in."

> neither of us was trying to learn to code. We were trying to impress each other. And we lost hours to it without noticing.

> "Once I learned how to program, I started seeing problems differently. Chemistry class: balance combustion equations by hand. Incredibly tedious. I thought — I could just... not. So I built a program that gives me the answer step by step.

By the end, i was able to combine all of these to Built a VR escape room. Each new skill you learn ultimately adds up to something new and cool that you can achieve.

## 6:30 — Being the Only Woman in the Room

> THis is a girls who code event, so I do also want to tell you about how it felt being the only woman. Obviously there's the robotics incident.

> "I started a UIL programming club. I remember wanting to recruit freshman women for the club but the same 3 freshman boys kept showing up
>
> "I went to BPA nationals for programming. I walked up to check in. The proctor looked down at his clipboard — didn't even glance up at me — and said: 'Please set up and take a seat in the room, sir.'"
>
> "I walked in. And I started counting the women."
>
> "Four. Total. All of them in the college division. I was the only high schooler."

> "And I want to be real with you: that's frustrating. It was frustrating then and it would be frustrating now. You're allowed to find it frustrating."

> However, being the "unique" person in the room, you really get to bring a diverse perspective. The frustration I felt, I was the only one feeling, and it gave me motivation that non of the other guys in the room could ever relate to.

## 8:00 — MIT

> "Continuing my coding journey - I ended up at MIT for electrical engineering and computer science and what i wasn't expecting was the crazy amount of diversity.

> For the first time ever, I'd do a scan in my classrooms and wouldn't feel uncomfortable. I wasn't counting. I just felt like I belonged. Everyone wanted to make something. Suddenly, this worry of being the only girl in the room disappeared and it really was like something was lifted off of my chest. Here, I was also given an amazing space to learn a ton about tech and go crazy with it.

_[App: MIT photo collage pushes to phones]_

> "Here are some fun things! One assignment in my security class: my professor basically said go break into something real. So my team hacked into the intro programming class. We could see and edit anyone's grades.

_[let them react]_

> "My identity very much changed in college. Instead of being "that girl who codes", I became that person who's curious across her set of interests — code, music, design, hardware — and finding cool places where they connect. Because that's where the interesting problems live. Not inside one field. At the edges and it's much easier to dig into something when you enjoy it."
